Natural Units

Picture a scenario where you're at a mall with your friend who just return from another country. How would you react if this friend saw the pricetag of a backpack and said "Can you imagine that I bought the exact same thing for 200 during my trip?"

I'm sure most people will be confused — "Which currency?" Without specifying the currency, this number "200" is meaningless; it could be extremely expensive or cheap.

The same problem happens in laboratories as well. If a scientist tells you that a device weighs "250," there is no way to judge whether the device is heavy or light unless we have prior knowledge about the device and/or the scientist.

Therefore, to avoid confusion, we need a way to provide people with an intuitive sense of how much the device weighs. One approach is to compare its weight to a familiar object, for example, a chicken. Instead of stating "250," the scientist could say that the device weighs 1/10 of a chicken's weight. If we have a rough idea of how much a chicken weighs, this comparison helps us estimate the device's weight easier.

Units

Therefore, to describe a quantity more efficiently, we need to compare it to something; and that "something" is the unit. In the previous example, to determine how much a device weighs, we compare it to the weight of a chicken. The chicken's weight is then the unit for measuring how heavy an object is.

For different quantities, we need different units. We wouldn’t measure the distance between Alice and Bob’s homes using the weight of a chicken, but we could compare it to the distance between Alice’s house and the local supermarket. For a time period, we could compare it to how long it takes for someone to take a shower. Similarly, to determine how fast a person runs, we could compare it to the speed of the dog that's chasing the person.

Nevertheless, the aforementioned units cannot serve as standard scientific units. For starters, we don't know which chicken was used as the unit for weight, the distance between a house and a local supermarket is not precise, a person's shower time can vary daily, and the dog's runing pace could depends on the reason for running. Secondly, these things or events do not last forever. Lastly, I don’t want to chase a chicken down just to figure out how much I weigh — both the chicken and I might have already lost some weight during the "exercise."
