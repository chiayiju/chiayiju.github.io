The General Solution of a Classical Closed String

Given the Lagrangian density
\[ {\cal L} = \frac{1}{2 \pi \ell_\text{s}} \left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \tau} X(\tau, \sigma)\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \sigma} X(\tau, \sigma)\right)^2\right], \]
where \(\ell_\text{s}\) is a constant that characterizes the typical size of the string, a quick calculation shows that the equation of motion derived from this Lagrangian density is
\begin{align} - \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \tau^2} X(\tau, \sigma) + \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \sigma^2} X(\tau, \sigma) = 0. \label{EOM} \end{align}
If we further impose the periodic condition
\[ X(\tau, \sigma + \pi) = X(\tau, \sigma), \]
the string is considered closed.

The general solution to the equation of motion under this condition is
\[ X(\tau, \sigma) = x + \ell_\text{s}^2 p \tau + i \ell_\text{s} \sum_{n \neq 0} \frac{1}{2 n} \left[a_n e^{-2 i n (\tau + \sigma)} + b_n e^{-2 i n (\tau - \sigma)}\right], \]
where \(x\), \(p\), \(a_n\), and \(b_n\) are arbitrary constants. In the following, we will demonstrate how this solution is obtained.

Step-by-Step Derivation

We begin with the equation of motion in Eq. (\ref{EOM}). However, rather than solving it by brute force, we make a change of variables, namely, \(\sigma^{\pm} = \tau \pm \sigma\). Then, the \(\tau\)- and \(\sigma\)-derivatives become
\[ \Rightarrow\left\{ \begin{array}{l} \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \tau} = \dfrac{\partial \sigma^+}{\partial \tau} \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^+} + \dfrac{\partial \sigma^-}{\partial \tau} \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^-} = \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^+} + \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^-}\\ \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma} = \dfrac{\partial \sigma^+}{\partial \sigma} \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^+} + \dfrac{\partial \sigma^-}{\partial \sigma} \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^-} = \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^+} - \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^-} \end{array} \right. . \]
Therefore, with a slight abuse of notation, the equation of motion becomes
\begin{align*} 0 & = - \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \tau^2} X + \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \sigma^2} X\\ & = - \left(\dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^+} + \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^-}\right)^2 X + \left(\dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^+} - \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^-}\right)^2 X\\ & = - 4 \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^+} \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^-} X. \end{align*}
We can solve for \(X\) by integrating both sides over \(\sigma^+\) and \(\sigma^-\), i.e.,
\begin{align*} X & = \int d\sigma^+ d\sigma^- \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^+} \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^-} X\\ & = \int d\sigma^+ d\sigma^- 0\\ & = \int d\sigma^+ F(\sigma^+)\\ & = X_\text{L}(\sigma^+) + X_\text{R}(\sigma^-), \end{align*}
where \(F(\sigma^+) = \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^+} X_\text{L}(\sigma^+)\), and \(X_\text{L}(\sigma^+)\) and \(X_\text{R}(\sigma^-)\) are arbitrary functions of \(\sigma^+\) and \(\sigma^-\), respectively. The subscript L and R stand for left-moving and right-moving modes.

Hence,
\[ X(\tau, \sigma) = X_\text{L}(\sigma^+) + X_\text{R}(\sigma^-) = X_\text{L}(\tau + \sigma) + X_\text{R}(\tau - \sigma). \]
Next, we impose the periodic condition \(X(\tau, \sigma + \pi) = X(\tau, \sigma)\), which leads to
\begin{align*} & X_\text{L}(\tau + \sigma + \pi) + X_\text{R}(\tau - \sigma - \pi) = X_\text{L}(\tau + \sigma) + X_\text{R}(\tau - \sigma)\\ \Rightarrow ~ & X_\text{L}(\sigma^+ + \pi) + X_\text{R}(\sigma^- - \pi) = X_\text{L}(\sigma^+) + X_\text{R}(\sigma^-). \end{align*}
Rearranging the equation gives:
\begin{align} X_\text{L}(\sigma^+ + \pi) - X_\text{L}(\sigma^+) = X_\text{R}(\sigma^-) - X_\text{R}(\sigma^- - \pi). \label{PeriodicConditions} \end{align}
Since \(\sigma^+\) and \(\sigma^-\) are independent variables and the right hand side of Eq. (\ref{PeriodicConditions}) does not depend on \(\sigma^+\), the left hand side must also be independent of \(\sigma^+\). Therefore,
\begin{align} X_\text{L}(\sigma^+ + \pi) - X_\text{L}(\sigma^+) = \pi \xi, \label{XL0} \end{align}
where \(\xi\) is an arbitrary constant. Consequently,
\begin{align} \Rightarrow X_\text{L}(\sigma^+ + n \pi) = n \pi \xi + X_\text{L}(\sigma^+). \label{XL} \end{align}
Similarly, the right-hand side of Eq. (\ref{PeriodicConditions}) is also equal \(\pi \xi\), i.e.,
\[ X_\text{R}(\sigma^-) - X_\text{R}(\sigma^- - \pi) = \pi \xi \]
\begin{align} \Rightarrow X_\text{R}(\sigma^- + \pi) - X_\text{R}(\sigma^-) = \pi \xi. \label{XR0} \end{align}
\begin{align} \Rightarrow X_\text{R}(\sigma^- + n \pi) = n \pi \xi + X_\text{R}(\sigma^-). \label{XR} \end{align}
Equations (\ref{XL}) and (\ref{XR}) suggest that both \(X_\text{L}(\sigma^-)\) and \(X_\text{R}(\sigma^-)\) contains linear terms. We therefore write
\[ \left\{ \begin{array}{l} X_\text{L}(\sigma^+) = \sigma^+ \xi + P_\text{L}(\sigma^+)\\ X_\text{R}(\sigma^-) = \sigma^- \xi + P_\text{R}(\sigma^-) \end{array} \right., \]
where \(P_\text{R}(\sigma^-)\) and \(P_\text{L}(\sigma^+)\) are arbitrary functions.

From Eqs. (\ref{XR0}) and (\ref{XL0}), it follows that \(P_\text{R}(\sigma^-)\) and \(P_\text{L}(\sigma^+)\) are periodic with period \(\pi\), i.e.,
\begin{align*} & \left\{ \begin{array}{l} X_\text{L}(\sigma^+ + \pi) - X_\text{L}(\sigma^+) = \pi \xi\\ X_\text{R}(\sigma^- + \pi) - X_\text{R}(\sigma^-) = \pi \xi \end{array} \right.\\ \Rightarrow & \left\{ \begin{array}{l} (\sigma^+ + \pi) \xi + P_\text{L}(\sigma^+ + \pi) - \left(\sigma^+ \xi + P_\text{L}(\sigma^+)\right) = \pi \xi\\ (\sigma^- + \pi) \xi + P_\text{R}(\sigma^- + \pi) - \left(\sigma^- \xi + P_\text{R}(\sigma^-)\right) = \pi \xi \end{array} \right. \\ \Rightarrow & \left\{ \begin{array}{l} P_\text{L}(\sigma^+ + \pi) = P_\text{L}(\sigma^+)\\ P_\text{R}(\sigma^- + \pi) = P_\text{R}(\sigma^-) \end{array} \right. . \end{align*}
Therefore, \(P_\text{R}(\sigma^-)\) and \(P_\text{L}(\sigma^+)\) can be expanded in Fourier series, namely,
\[ \left\{ \begin{array}{l} \displaystyle P_\text{L}(\sigma^+) = \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty} \alpha_n e^{- 2 i n \sigma^+}\\ \displaystyle P_\text{R}(\sigma^-) = \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty} \beta_n e^{- 2 i n \sigma^-} \end{array} \right. , \]
where \(\alpha_n\) and \(\beta_n\) are arbitrary constants. Putting everything together, we find
\begin{align*} X(\tau, \sigma) & = \sigma^+ \pi \xi + \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty} \alpha_n e^{- 2 i n \sigma^+} + \sigma^- \pi \xi + \sum_{m = - \infty}^{\infty} \beta_m e^{- 2 i m \sigma^-}\\ & = (\sigma^+ + \sigma^-) \pi \xi + \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty} \left(\alpha_n e^{- 2 i n \sigma^+} + \beta_n e^{- 2 i n \sigma^-}\right)\\ & = \tau \pi \xi + \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty} \left(\alpha_n e^{- 2 i n (\tau + \sigma)} + \beta_n e^{- 2 i n (\tau - \sigma)}\right)\\ & = \tau \pi \xi + \alpha_0 + \beta_0 + \sum_{n \neq 0} \left(\alpha_n e^{- 2 i n (\tau + \sigma)} + \beta_n e^{- 2 i n (\tau - \sigma)}\right), \end{align*}
where we have separated out the \(n = 0\) terms in the last equality. Since \(\xi\), \(\alpha_n\), \(\beta_n\) are arbitrary constants, we redefine the constants as
\[ \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} \displaystyle \xi = \frac{\ell_\text{s}^2 p}{\pi}\\ \alpha_0 + \beta_0 = x\\ \displaystyle \alpha_n = \frac{i \ell_\text{s}}{2 n} a_n & \text{(for \(n \neq 0\))}\\ \displaystyle \beta_n = \frac{i \ell_\text{s}}{2 n} b_n & \text{(for \(n \neq 0\))} \end{array} \right. . \]
Finally, we arrived at the general solution:
\[ X(\tau, \sigma) = x + \ell_\text{s}^2 p \tau + i \ell_\text{s} \sum_{n \neq 0} \frac{1}{2 n} \left[a_n e^{-2 i n (\tau + \sigma)} + b_n e^{-2 i n (\tau - \sigma)}\right]. \]
